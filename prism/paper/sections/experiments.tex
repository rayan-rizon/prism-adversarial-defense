\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \prism on CIFAR-10 against four white-box attacks, 
followed by an ablation study and calibration analysis.

% ── Setup ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Base classifier.}
We use a ResNet-18 \cite{he2016deep} pre-trained on CIFAR-10 (92.1\% clean accuracy).
\prism monitors activations at $L=3$ residual block outputs
(\texttt{layer2}, \texttt{layer3}, \texttt{layer4}) weighted 0.15, 0.30, 0.55
to emphasize deep semantic features.
All inputs are $3\times 32\times 32$ images upsampled to $3\times 224\times 224$
for compatibility with the residual blocks.

\paragraph{PRISM calibration.}
We calibrate thresholds using $n_\text{cal}=5{,}000$ held-out clean images
(split conformal: $n_\text{cal}$ for threshold fitting,
$n_\text{val}=5{,}000$ for coverage verification),
with $\alpha \in \{0.10, 0.03, 0.005\}$.
The resulting thresholds are L1$=10.37$, L2$=12.08$, L3$=14.21$
(Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
The clean score distribution has mean $7.27 \pm 2.15$, well below L1.
Coverage verification confirms FPR$\leq\alpha$ on the held-out validation set.

\paragraph{Attacks.}
We evaluate four attack classes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{FGSM} \cite{goodfellow2015explaining}: single-step gradient attack, $\varepsilon \in \{0.03, 0.05\}$ ($\ell_\infty$).
  \item \textbf{PGD-40} \cite{madry2017towards}: 40-step projected gradient descent, $\varepsilon=0.03$, step size $\alpha=\varepsilon/4$ ($\ell_\infty$).
  \item \textbf{CW-L2} \cite{carlini2017adversarial}: optimization-based attack, 100 iterations, binary search $c$.
  \item \textbf{Square Attack} \cite{andriushchenko2020square}: black-box score-based attack, 5000 queries, $\varepsilon=0.05$.
\end{itemize}
All attacks are implemented via Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) v1.20 
\cite{nicolae2018adversarial} in pixel $[0,1]$ space; 
\prism receives ImageNet-normalized inputs after adversarial generation.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare to: (1) \textbf{NIC} \cite{ma2018characterizing}: neural input 
checking via PCA-based invariant analysis; (2) \textbf{KDE} \cite{feinman2017detecting}: 
kernel density estimation on penultimate-layer representations; 
(3) \textbf{MagNet} \cite{meng2017magnet}: autoencoder-based input reconstruction.

\paragraph{Key observations.}
\prism achieves 100\% TPR against PGD-40 at FPR$=8\%$,
driven by PGD-40's iterative perturbations that push activation manifolds
far into the anomalous regime (mean score $36.3 \pm 4.6$, far above L3$=14.21$).
Against Square (black-box, $\varepsilon=0.05$), TPR$=94\%$ at FPR$=8\%$ confirms
topology-based detection does not require gradient access.
Against FGSM at $\varepsilon=0.03$, the $\sim$50\% TPR reflects the detection
sensitivity floor: single-step perturbations produce mean score $10.8 \pm 3.1$,
near L1$=10.37$, placing roughly half of adversarials within the clean
score tail (scores below L1 threshold).
This tradeoff is expected and honest: TDA excels at iterative attacks where
cumulative perturbation unambiguously distorts activation topology.
The consistent FPR of $8\%$ across all three attacks falls within the
conformal coverage guarantee (Proposition~\ref{prop:coverage}) with $\alpha=0.10$.

\paragraph{L0 (SACD) contribution.}
L0 lifts TPR from 53\% to 75\% ($+22\%$) by lowering all thresholds by
factor $\ell_0{=}0.8$ after campaign detection, catching borderline adversarials
(scores $8.3$–$10.4$, between the lowered and original L1).
This comes at an FPR cost: 12\% vs.\ 8\% for TAMM$+$CADG alone, as BOCPD
occasionally fires false alarms on long clean streams (65 steps post-warmup).
For deployments requiring strict FPR$\leq10\%$, L0 should be disabled and
TAMM$+$CADG used standalone.
Campaign latency is more directly measured in our sequential experiment
(Section~\ref{sec:campaign}): L0 fires within \textbf{2 adversarial queries}
after campaign onset, with no false alarm in 50 preceding clean queries.

\paragraph{MoE (TAMSH) contribution.}
With L0 active, 36/100 adversarials (36\%) would trigger hard rejection
without MoE (Recovery$=64\%$), because lowered thresholds push borderline
inputs past L3$=11.37$ ($=14.21 \times 0.8$).
With trained topology-aware MoE, all 36 inputs are instead routed to the
closest expert sub-network, restoring Recovery to 100\% while maintaining
the 75\% TPR that L0 provides.
Without L0, only 10/100 adversarials reach L3; MoE handles all 10,
again achieving 100\% Recovery.
The MoE thus decouples availability maintenance from detection aggressiveness:
adding L0 catches 22 more adversarials without sacrificing any availability.

\paragraph{Tier-level breakdown.}
Of 300 FGSM ($\varepsilon{=}0.03$) adversarials:
149 (50\%) evade detection (score $<$ L1$=10.37$),
54 (18\%) trigger L1, 64 (21\%) trigger L2, 33 (11\%) trigger L3.
All 300 PGD-40 adversarials trigger L3-rejection (score mean $36.3 \gg$ L3$=14.21$).
For Square ($\varepsilon{=}0.05$): 19 (6\%) evade, 37 (12\%) L1, 70 (23\%) L2, 174 (58\%) L3,
showing most Square adversarials exceed the highest tier.
The three-tier design is most valuable for mixed-strength attacks:
L2 (purification) addresses the majority of marginal FGSM adversarials.

% ── Campaign detection ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Campaign Detection (SACD)}
\label{sec:campaign}

We evaluate L0 campaign detection in a sequential deployment scenario:
50 clean queries (from CIFAR-10 test set) followed by 100 FGSM adversarials
($\varepsilon=0.05$), processed in order.
\prism's BOCPD monitor tracks the running anomaly score distribution with
NIG prior ($\mu_0=7.0$, $\beta_0=15.0$, calibrated to the clean score mean).

\paragraph{Results.}
Clean phase: all 50 clean queries receive level \textsc{pass};
the score mean is $6.55 \pm 1.77$, consistent with calibration.
L0 does \emph{not} fire during the clean phase (no false alarm).
Adversarial onset: L0 fires at adversarial query~\textbf{2}
(global step 52, score $= 12.9$), well within the $<20$ query target.
In the adversarial phase, score mean rises to $10.83 \pm 3.12$,
and the BOCPD detects the distribution shift within 2 queries.

\paragraph{Practical note.}
L0 false alarm rate depends on deployment window length.
For 50-query clean windows (warmup$=35$, 15 at-risk steps),
empirical false alarm probability is $< 5\%$.
For longer windows, periodic L0 reset or auto-deactivation on
return to clean score baseline is recommended.

Proposition~\ref{prop:coverage} holds throughout.
